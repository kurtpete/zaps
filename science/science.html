Two-dimensional paper chromatography can separate a mixture of unknown molecules for identification. A sample is placed at the bottom of a strip of paper, which is dipped into a solvent. Solvent travels up the paper, dissolving and carrying the sample. Molecules that are weakly attracted to the solvent are soon deposited on the paper. Molecules that cling strongly are carried farther. Figure 1 shows the direction of travel.
To further separate the sample, the paper is turned on its side and dipped in a second solvent. This liquid separates spots deposited by the first solvent. Figure 2 shows the direction of travel.
Below is a chromatogram showing the results of an experiment that separated chemicals involved in photosynthesis. Algae photosynthesized for 30 seconds, then were killed and blended in a blender. An algae sample was placed at the lower right hand corner of the paper. The bottom of the paper was dipped into a butanol solvent, and the sample was separated. DNA, found in the nucleus of the cell, carries genetic codes for proteins. When a cell makes new proteins, DNA’s double helix unwinds, and a segment of a single strand of DNA is copied. The copy is not made of DNA; it is made of a molecule called RNA. RNA leaves the nucleus, and other organelles in the cell use the RNA as a template for building a new protein. Researchers debate how new proteins wind up in the parts of the cell where they are to be used.
Researcher 1 claims that after a strand of RNA leaves the nucleus, it is placed in a capsule called a vesicle and attached to part of the cytoskeleton (the network of fibers and rods that gives the cell shape and acts as a railway system for particles moving throughout the cell). Once attached to the cytoskeleton, RNA moves to the location where the new protein is to be used. When a strand of RNA reaches its destination, it is used as the template for building the new protein.
Researcher 2 argues that after a strand of RNA leaves the nucleus, it floats freely to a nearby organelle called the endoplasmic reticulum. The endoplasmic reticulum is studded with protein-building bodies called ribosomes. The ribosomes use RNA as a template for building the new proteins. After the proteins are made, they are packaged into vesicles, attached to the cytoskeleton, and transported to the places where they will be used.

The best answer is A. Spinach juice produces a milliliter of gas in about 0.25 seconds, while potato juice produces that volume in 1 second. Since the O2 is said to come from H2O2 decomposition, it is fair to say that spinach juice is decomposing H2O2 at 4 times potato's rate.

The best answer is not B. Broccoli decomposes H202 about four times more slowly than kale does. A lower number indicates more rapid decomposition than a higher number.

The best answer is not C. Peppers produce O2 more slowly than any other vegetable juice tested, suggesting that it has the lowest concentration of catalase.

The best answer is not D. Potato juice produces O2 more rapidly than carrot juice does, suggesting potato juice has a higher concentration of catalase.”

Optical density is a measure of how much light changes speed as it passes through a material. The higher a material's optical density, the more it refracts, or changes the direction of, light as light enters from the air.
To determine relative optical densities, a group of students measured angles of refraction in four materials: water, clear gelatin, and two mystery substances, A and B. They placed clear glass containers holding each substance against a wall, on a sheet of graph paper with coordinates marked. They shined a ruby laser at an angle of 45° to the side of the container. The beam of light refracted in the material and struck a different point on the wall for each of the materials.
